# Peripheral-Theoretic Critique of "Implementing the learning from training in Open Research practices"

## What the paper does

The paper develops a logic model of open research culture change using
Normalisation Process Theory (NPT), grounded in interview data from a
UK-wide Train-the-Trainer programme. Five design patterns are identified
(Confident Adaptation, Protected Interaction, Shared Maintenance,
Contribution Pathways, Visible Impact) and composed into three
implementation modes (multiplied input, absorbed input, architectural
mismatch). The theoretical framing draws on Alexander (pattern language),
Sen (capability approach), and Ostrom (institutional analysis / commons
governance).

The paper is explicitly *pattern-theoretic*: it uses Alexander's
context–tension–resolution form, and its patterns are conditional
programme claims. It is not yet explicitly *peripheral-theoretic*. This
critique reads the paper through the three peripherals patterns and
identifies where the peripheral framing adds explanatory power.

---

## Reading 1: The T3 programme as a constrained execution envelope

**Pattern applied:** `peripherals/constrained-execution-envelope`

The paper's Pattern 1 (Confident Adaptation) is about trainers needing to
know "what is fixed and what is flexible." The resolution is "not rigidity
but clarity — a minimal scaffold that makes local adaptation safe."

This is exactly the constrained execution envelope. The T3 programme is
(or should be) a peripheral that trainers inhabit: a space where the
available actions (deliver these topics, adapt within this scope, use
these materials) constitute the productive path. When the envelope is
well-defined, trainers adapt confidently. When it isn't, they experience
the failure mode the pattern predicts: "envelope too loose — the available
actions include tangential work that feels productive."

The paper's T2A trainer who expected a "pass-through model" and found she
had to "put the things together" is experiencing an envelope that was
never communicated as an envelope. She didn't know she was supposed to
inhabit a formative space; she thought she was receiving a package.

**Insight for the paper:** The T3 programme can be explicitly framed as a
constrained execution envelope for training delivery. The "scaffold" the
paper calls for is the positive description of what the envelope contains
— not a list of prohibitions or requirements, but a description of the
actions that constitute good delivery. The paper already gestures at this
("clarity, not rigidity") but doesn't use the envelope language, which
would make the design recommendation more precise: define the envelope in
positive terms (here are the tools, phases, and exit conditions for
delivery), not in negative terms (here are the things you must not skip).

**Nietzsche connection the paper doesn't make:** The paper's discussion of
"expectations" is framed as an information problem (trainers didn't know
what was required). The envelope pattern reframes it as an *affordance*
problem: the issue isn't that trainers lacked information about what they
couldn't do, but that they lacked a positive description of what they
could do. This is the Nietzschean point — prohibition doesn't work;
affirmation does.

## Reading 2: The institutional architecture as a surface that must earn inhabitation

**Pattern applied:** `peripherals/surface-earns-inhabitation`

The paper's central finding is that "motivation was high across all three
modes — what varied was the institutional architecture." Trainers wanted
to do the work. They had the skills. But the surface — the institutional
setting where the work would happen — was cluttered, difficult to enter,
or unsupported. So they didn't fully inhabit it.

This is the surface-earns-inhabitation pattern applied at the
institutional scale. The paper documents exactly the death spiral:

- The "multiplied input" mode is the case where the surface earned
  inhabitation: "I have integrated it into existing structures of content
  delivery that I'm involved with." The trainer inhabited the surface
  because the surface was ready.

- The "absorbed input" mode is the case where the surface was inhabitable
  but fragile: the trainer carried the programme alone, filling structural
  gaps with goodwill. The surface didn't earn inhabitation — the
  individual forced it.

- The "architectural mismatch" mode is the case where the surface was
  uninhabitable: "I struggled actually to fit everything together."

**Insight for the paper:** The paper already has the right diagnosis
("what varied was the institutional architecture") but frames it as an
*alignment* problem. The peripheral framing adds a dynamic: the surface
must earn inhabitation, and it earns it by being less friction than the
alternative. What is the alternative in the T3 case? Not doing the
training. Not adapting the materials. Not delivering locally. The path of
least resistance is always to let the programme lapse. The institutional
architecture must make *continuing* easier than *stopping* — which is a
stronger claim than "alignment."

**Ostrom connection the paper could sharpen:** The paper already uses
Ostrom for commons governance (Pattern 3: Shared Maintenance). But the
surface-earns-inhabitation pattern suggests a deeper Ostrom connection:
the institution is the *action arena*, and the question is whether its
rules-in-use (not rules-on-paper) make participation the natural path.
Ostrom's design principles for long-enduring commons institutions
(clearly defined boundaries, congruence between rules and local
conditions, collective-choice arrangements, monitoring) are literally
the conditions under which the institutional surface earns inhabitation.

## Reading 3: The missing Baldwin loop

**Pattern applied:** `peripherals/inhabitation-feeds-evolution`

The paper's Pattern 5 (Visible Impact) identifies a critical gap: "the
programme cannot demonstrate impact to the institutions it depends on for
support." Feedback mechanisms are thin. Cross-institutional evaluation is
absent in all four transcripts.

This is the Baldwin loop, starved. The paper documents the exact failure
mode: no inhabitation data → no programme evolution → no improvement →
continued low inhabitation. But it frames this as a "monitoring problem"
(Ostrom) rather than as a learning-loop problem.

The peripheral framing adds something: the feedback gap isn't just about
*accountability* (can we demonstrate impact?) — it's about *evolution*
(can the programme reshape itself based on usage data?). The paper's
Pattern 5 is framed as the programme needing evidence to *justify* itself.
The Baldwin pattern says the programme needs evidence to *improve* itself
— and that the improvement is what earns further inhabitation.

**The upsampling insight applied to T3:** The paper notes that local
adaptations were made but not systematically shared (Pattern 3). In
Baldwin terms, the social-loop learning (trainers adapting materials
locally) never fed back into the glacial loop (programme-level design).
The paper proposes "distributed stewardship" as the fix, which is
correct, but the Baldwin framing makes the *urgency* clearer: every
session delivered without feedback flowing back into the programme is a
session whose learning is lost. The programme's evolution is being
starved at the rate of its own delivery.

**The symmetry the paper hasn't noticed:** The paper treats trainers and
the programme as separate entities — trainers inhabit the programme, the
programme provides structure. The Baldwin pattern reveals they're in a
co-evolutionary loop. When trainers adapt materials (social loop), that
adaptation is data about what works locally. When the programme
incorporates that data (glacial loop), the next generation of trainers
gets a better envelope. The programme literally evolves through its
inhabitants' actions — but only if those actions are captured and fed
back. This is exactly what the paper's "contribution pathways" (Pattern 4)
and "visible impact" (Pattern 5) are trying to create, but the paper
doesn't frame them as two halves of a single evolutionary loop.

## Reading 4: The coordination gradient as envelope nesting

The paper's most striking structural finding is the coordination gradient
(Table 3): patterns require progressively more actors in longer chains,
from 1 (Confident Adaptation) to 6+ (Visible Impact).

In peripheral terms, this is envelope nesting. Each pattern is a
peripheral that encloses a wider action space:

| Pattern | Envelope | Who inhabits |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Confident Adaptation | Training Project defines the scaffold | Programme designers |
| Protected Interaction | Institution provides space + support | Trainer + institution |
| Shared Maintenance | Community stewards materials | Contributor community |
| Contribution Pathways | Infrastructure enables co-ownership | CoP + coordinators |
| Visible Impact | Evaluation chain produces feedback | 6+ actor chain |

Each envelope must earn inhabitation independently. The paper's finding
that "the programme was most fragile where the coordination chain was
longest" is precisely the prediction of surface-earns-inhabitation: the
more actors required, the more surfaces that must independently earn
inhabitation, and the more likely the chain breaks.

**Insight for the paper:** The paper frames the gradient as a "difficulty"
gradient — harder patterns require more coordination. The peripheral
framing reframes it as a *nested inhabitation* gradient: each pattern is
an envelope that must be independently earned. The failure mode isn't
"too hard" but "too many surfaces that haven't earned their inhabitants."
This shifts the design recommendation from "simplify the coordination"
to "make each surface independently inhabitable before relying on the
chain."

## Summary: What peripheral theory adds to the paper

| Paper concept | Peripheral reframing | What it adds |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Expectations scaffold | Constrained execution envelope | Positive affordances, not information about requirements |
| Institutional alignment | Surface earns inhabitation | Dynamic: must be less friction than the alternative |
| Feedback gap | Baldwin loop starved | Not just accountability but programme evolution |
| Coordination gradient | Nested envelope inhabitation | Each surface must earn independently |
| "Absorbed input" mode | Forced inhabitation | Individual fills structural gap; surface not earned |
| Culture change | Co-evolutionary loop | Programme and inhabitants evolve together, or neither does |

The paper is close to being peripheral-theoretic. Its patterns are
already constrained execution envelopes (they define productive action
spaces). Its implementation modes are already inhabitation outcomes (they
describe who entered and who didn't). Its feedback gap is already a
starved Baldwin loop. What's missing is the framing that unifies these
observations: the programme is a peripheral, institutions are surfaces,
trainers are inhabitants, and the whole system is a co-evolutionary loop
that runs only when inhabited.

The deepest addition is the Baldwin insight: the paper treats programme
design as static (design it well, then deliver) when the peripheral
framing shows it must be dynamic (deliver it, learn from delivery, evolve
the design, deliver again). The paper already knows this — Pattern 5 is
about feedback — but doesn't yet frame feedback as the engine of
programme evolution rather than merely the evidence of programme impact.
