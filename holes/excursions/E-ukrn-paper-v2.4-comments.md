## Candidate new patterns

Four candidate-new patterns surfaced in this annotation pass. Each is named in kebab-case; promotion to `library/writing-coherence/` is Joe's call.

### `section-bridge-missing` (Thread 20)
**Gloss.** A paragraph that closes a section reads as if it were a finding rather than a positioning move, and the next section begins without a connective sentence. The reader cannot tell whether the closing claim was the section's conclusion or its setup; the section-to-section connective tissue has been silently elided. Distinct from `throat-clearing-close` (which is about ending on attitude rather than claim) and from `meta-lede` (which is about opening on shape rather than content): here the diagnostic is the *transition*, not the sentence in isolation. Composition: name what's been established and what's about to be addressed in one bridge sentence.

### `first-mention-undischarged` (Threads 22, 32, and arguably 18)
**Gloss.** A domain-specific named entity (ORP, ORCA, "stewards", "the Training Project") is invoked before the paragraph that defines it. This is structurally different from `floating-formalism` (typed vocabulary without instances) and from `plain-language-thesis` (jargon-reliance at thesis position): the term *will* be defined later, but the reader has to carry an unresolved name through several paragraphs. Composition: introduce the entity in plain language at first mention, even if a fuller treatment lives in a later section.

### `cross-section-claim-drift` (Thread 45)
**Gloss.** The same factual claim is made in two sections of a paper using different wording, with no single canonical formulation. Methods says "X for purposes A, B, C"; Limitations says "X for purposes A, B" — reviewers read the discrepancy as inconsistency even when the meaning is identical. Adjacent to `paraphrase-drift` (which is intra-paragraph elegant-restatement) but operates at the section level and concerns *factual* alignment, not rhetorical advancement. Composition: choose one canonical wording for the load-bearing claim and reuse it verbatim in every section that invokes it.

### `structural-style-inconsistency` (Thread 31)
**Gloss.** Headings, sub-headings, and run-in bold labels mix multiple formatting conventions within the same document, so that a reader scanning the table of contents or skim-reading cannot infer hierarchy. This is downstream of journal-style ambiguity but it materially affects argument legibility independently of the journal target. Distinct from `subheading-without-paragraph` (which is about whether the subheading is *discharged* by its body) — here the body is fine but the *form* of the subheading drifts. Composition: choose one heading-level convention per logical depth, normalise punctuation and capitalisation, and audit the file from top to bottom in one pass.
